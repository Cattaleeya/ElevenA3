Project

Overview

This project aims to create an automated indoor plant-care system for everyday consumers, with a modern design that helps it to seamlessly blend in with the surrounding decor making it a feature of the home, rather than an eye-sore, and a multi-device capable user-friendly control system application that takes the guesswork out of plant-parenting.

By utilising and expanding on existing irrigation systems and open-source plant-care software we can create a product that will become an essential part of indoor gardening and remove the worry of trying to find a plant-sitter or having to leave your plants unattended when you are away for extended periods of time.

Motivation

Since the beginning of the Covid19 pandemic, sales of indoor plants have increased exponentially. While the trend of owning indoor plants was already on the rise, the extended isolation periods and work-from-home orders due to Covid19 has compelled many individuals to take up ‘plant parenting’ as a hobby, for relaxation and enjoyment, as well as purifying the air in their homes, and creating a way to stay connected with nature while unable to leave the house (Pflughoeft 2021). This booming trend, also known as ‘the Millennial plant revolution’ (George 2020) has seen rise of inexperienced but hopeful house-planters who are now becoming frustrated at the expense and time they are putting into their new plants only to have them wilt and die from overwatering or being scorched, and then trying multitudes of remedies to reverse the damage, creating a cycle of confusion, and not knowing what else to do (Sullivan 2021).

Additionally, as the world begins to open back up, these new ‘plant-parents’, along with existing indoor plant enthusiasts, are going to find themselves faced with the challenge of keeping their plants alive when they are no longer home as often to care for them. Holidays, work trips, and long workdays can all contribute to the accidental neglect of house plants, with many forgetting to keep up with the watering, or returning home from a trip to dehydrated or dead plants. This leaves people needing to find a way to revive their plants or buy new plants each time they accidentally kill one.

In order to prevent the untimely demise of house plants, we are creating an automated indoor plant-care system that will allow wireless monitoring and watering of potted plants, with both manual and automatic settings. It is to be designed in such a way that allows you to easily accommodate different sizes and types of plants without needing to be an expert gardener. This will be especially beneficial to those wanting to ensure their plants are cared for while they are away from home, but also serve as an everyday set-and-forget care system, giving you one less thing to worry about

Description

There are a variety of existing automatic indoor irrigation systems on the market today, but there is always something lacking from them. Most current types will only function as a single station with one main water line, meaning that while it may have a split waterline that can be used for multiple plants, it all comes from one system and distributes the same amount of water to each plant at the same rate. The problem with this is the lack of accommodation for not only varying pot sizes, but varying water consumption requirements for individual plant types as well.

For example, if you have a large fern in a 30cm pot, and a small begonia in a 12cm pot, they will both be supplied with the same amount of water, causing either one plant to be overwatered, or one to be underwatered. Similarly, a succulent and an African violet both in the same size pots will also have different watering level and frequency requirements

To solve this problem, you need multiple valves in addition to multiple water lines that can be calibrated and operated individually by one main reservoir system. The task of getting water into the pots at individual rates is not the only challenge, as each pot may not always need the same amount of water dispersed each time. So, to fine tune the amount and rate of water dispersed you will need to monitor the moisture levels of the plants to determine the optimal time and amount needed for repeated watering. You will also need to consider the individual plants growing cycle, maturity, and the season or time of year. Additionally, if you are away from home, you will not have the ability to open or close blinds or move plants in and out of sun or shade, so with the inclusion of dimmable, colouring changing LED lighting over each pot station you can ensure your plants are getting an optimal amount of light to keep them healthy when blinds remained closed or simply over winter

We can further reduce the steps required in the care process by producing custom pots with integrated moisture sensors, taking away the need for the user to insert a sensor into the plant soil themselves, never knowing if they have put it in the right spot, or potentially damaging the plant in the process. Additionally, each of these pots can be Wi-Fi enabled, allowing the main system to recognise which pot it is, and being able to transmit the monitoring data to the application for the user. If the user chooses to use the system with their own pot instead of one of our smart pots, they will still need to manually insert a moisture sensor with wireless connectivity, and a watering line will need to be placed in the soil directly. The individual pot designs can be based on one of the existing open-source self-watering pot systems, such as the one created by YouTuber ‘DIY Machines’ (2019), however these pots only hold a limited amount of water in their individual reservoir, and still require manual filling when it runs out. This type of smart pot system could be adapted to sit on a base at the main station which will refill the small reservoir from the main reservoir, allowing it the plant continue to self-water for extended periods of time.

The control system application will allow you to create individual plant-specific watering and sunlight schedules while providing you with feedback on your plant’s condition through real-time monitoring. This means that users can monitor, set up and customise their plant’s care schedule from anywhere in the world via a smart phone or device with the aid of Raspberry Pi technology (Hallek 2020).

Such applications already exist in various forms but are not as user-friendly as they could be. We would design an application that will make calculated suggestions on optimal watering times and amounts based on the monitored data. The base system itself will initially have the capacity to accommodate 5 pots. It will consist of a larger reservoir of 7 litres which can be used to refill the individual pot reservoir, or for plants in a traditional pot the refill line can use a manual insertion adapter to place the sensors and water line directly in the pot. The smart pots can be wirelessly paired to the system application and where the user can set the plant name and preferred moisture content or watering frequency. The system will then operate the individual water lines connected to the plants. Each of the 5 pot stations will have an LED light above them to provide light to all or individual plants, controlled again by the app with timer options.

A feature of this system is the ability to continue using the automatic self-watering pots even when home with the plants. Once home, the user can place the pots around the house where they choose and instead of the base station refilling the pot, the app will instead alert the user to manually refill the pot’s smaller reservoir, meaning that plant positioning is not limited to the base.

In general, all systems of automation have both advantages and disadvantages that need to be considered when deciding on which solution to use, and not all systems will be suitable for every property. Space, water access, power access and Wi-Fi connectivity will be required in order to make use of the system we are designing. However, if these requirements are met, the user will be able to go on holiday and travel care-free without needing to hire a house-sitter or worrying about how they will meet the needs of their beloved plants while they are gone.

Tools & Tech

The following list of tools and technologies are what we have determined will be required for this project. This is not an exhaustive list and may be subject to change as more details about the project are formed.

  • Raspberry Pi (model to be determined)
  • Electronic breadboard
  • Several Solenoid valves and relays
  • Several moisture sensors
  • Electrical wiring
  • Battery
  • Switches
  • LCD display panel
  • Water reservoir
  • Water tubing
  • Water level sensors
  • Python programming language
  • Raspberry Pi OS
  • LED lighting

Additionally, we will require a casing/housing for the main watering station, as well as pots (these could be 3D printed to specification), hosting platform to run the app, a smart phone for testing the application and a willing indoor plant to test the watering system on.

Skills

To build this specific project, you need to have some basic skills and knowledge in the Python programming language, application and UX design, electronic engineering, experience using Raspberry Pi, knowledge of irrigation systems, as well as some botanical or horticultural knowledge.

Where skills in these areas are lacking, we can use research skills to obtain the knowledge we need, or failing that we can seek advice, mentoring, or outsource various aspects that are beyond the current abilities of our team.

Outcome

The result of the project should be a functioning automated watering system with the capability to be controlled wirelessly with both automatic scheduling and manual ‘on-demand’ watering and lighting capabilities.

Ultimately the outcome is going to be having the reassurance to be able to leave indoor plants unattended for an extended period of time, knowing that their individual watering requirements are going to be automatically taken care of.

The limitation of this system will be the size of the water reservoir and will determine the length of time that plants can be left unattended. The use of a reservoir is the initial choice over a plumed to faucet solution for easier set-up and manoeuvrability; however this could also be a future implementation.
