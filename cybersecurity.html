Cybersecurity

How many of these terms have you heard of? Digital footprint, web browsing, hacking, viruses, malware, spyware, authentication, password security, trojans, programming, brute force attack, cyber-attacks, firewalls, cookies. These are all terms used when talking about Cybersecurity but let us start by discussing what Cybersecurity is.

The word ‘Cyber’ refers to anything related to computing, information technology, and the internet, and Cybersecurity is “the technologies and processes designed to protect the computers, networks and data from unauthorized access.” (Beal 1996). Potential instances of unauthorized access are known as cybersecurity threats. There are several types of cybersecurity threats, including Phishing - email and text scams; Ransomware - malicious software that holds your data hostage and requests a payment; and Malware – software that gains unauthorized access to your computer, potentially stealing data or damaging files (Cisco, n.d.).

In an annual cybersecurity survey by Accenture, it was revealed that cyberattacks on businesses have risen by 31% from 2020 – 2021, globally, with an average of 270 attacks per business over the 12-month period. These survey results highlight the importance of implementing a sound cybersecurity strategy, especially where critical infrastructure and cloud security is concerned (Bissel et al. 2021).

Areas of Cybersecurity

There are 5 main categories of cybersecurity; Application security, Critical infrastructure security, Cloud security, IoT (Internet of Things) security, and Network security (CompTIA n.d).

Application Security

Application security refers to protecting application code from being maliciously accessed, stolen, or overwritten, both during the development process and after deployment. Methods such as user authentication, access authorisation, data encryption, activity logging, and security testing are used to minimise security weaknesses (VMware n.d.).

Critical Infrastructure Security

Critical infrastructure security is the protection of essential physical systems, such as telephone networks, electrical grids, traffic lights, workstation hardware, and hospitals, that businesses and society depend on. When these services become connected to third parties, via networking and the Internet, they become vulnerable to cyberattacks. (CMIT Solutions, n.d.)

Cloud Security

Cloud security is the protection of cloud-based data, systems, and infrastructure from internal and external cyberthreats through the implementation of technology, policies, controls, and procedures, with responsibility shared between the cloud service provider and the user, depending on the service level they are using (Forcepoint n.d.).

IoT Security

IoT (Internet of Things) security is the process of securing, monitoring and protecting electronic devices that have network and internet connectivity. Devices such as printers, smart appliances, medical imaging systems and security cameras are some of the highest-ranking devices in terms of security vulnerability, and 98% of IoT device traffic is being sent unencrypted, making it essential to include IoT security as part of an overall cybersecurity plan. (Palo Alto Networks, n.d.)

Network Security

Network security refers to the implementation of actions, processes, and policies for protecting the usability and integrity of data networks through monitoring, detection, and prevention methods controlling the authorisation of access to public or private networks (Cisco n.d.).

Cybersecurity Measures

How can you protect information from cybersecurity threats?

Strong Passwords

A password should ideally be between 8-12 characters long and use a combination of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols. Change your passwords regularly, avoid using the same password for multiple accounts and do not share your password with others (NI Business Info n.d.).

Two-factor authentication

Protect passwords further by requiring an additional login code from a separate device or email account (NI Business Info n.d.).

Access control

Controls who has access to what data by managing account security access levels, keeping passwords stored safe, and limiting access to premises or devices so only authorised persons can gain access to them. (NI Business Info n.d.)

Firewalls

A firewall is a network security device in the form of software or hardware, that acts as a filter or barrier between your computer and the incoming data. When configured correctly it can protect you from malicious software being installed on your computer and prevent unauthorized access. (Johansen 2021)

Security software

Internet security software provides options for automated monitoring, identification, quarantining and removal of malicious software and prevents unauthorised changes from being made to your computer by hackers, malware, spyware, ransomware, and viruses. The benefits of using a security software service include having regular updates to the library of threats they search for, as this is constantly evolving (Cisco 2021).

Minimise your digital footprint

A digital footprint is the trail of information left behind by your online activities and interactions. Once your information is online it may be there permanently, and you might not even realise the amount or types of data you have left behind (Kaspersky n.d.)

How many websites have you visited today? How many social media accounts do you have?

Have you ever noticed how when you Google something - perfume, for example - you start to notice ads about that product, on your Facebook feed, on your YouTube videos, or random ads on the news page you may go to? This is because in each website there is code in there called meta data that the website owners are performing analytics on. By analysing the visitor data on a website, organisations can gather a huge amount of information. From how long a potential customer stayed on the site, what they clicked on, or what search terms they used.

Additionally, images you upload, statuses you make and posts you comment on while online can be used to gather information about you for malicious purposes. Someone may be able to piece together where you live, your daily schedule, places you like to visit, as well as your favourite colour, your pets' names, your mother's family name – information that can be used to try and gain access to your accounts and email or steal your identity.

Understand what you are trying to protect

What online activities do you do or use?

  • Online banking
  • Social media (eg. Twitter, Facebook)
  • Online shopping
  • Watch videos on YouTube
  • Streaming services (eg. Disney+, Stan)
  • Email services (eg. Gmail, Outlook)
  • Dating sites

Which anti-virus / internet protection software should you use on your computer?

Your answer to this may be, ‘well I have AVG, it was free, that should protect me’. Well, dig a little deeper. If it is just an anti-virus, is it only protecting again “viruses”? What about malware, spyware, internet banking, password protection?

How many devices are connected to your network at home? Think of gaming consoles, smart watches, Google mini’s, smart Tv’s, printers and washing machines.

Does your phone have protection? Does your tablet/iPad have protection?

Would basic anti-virus software be enough? or do you need a full suite of network security?

When researching what solution to use, you should consider these questions and compare them against the levels and types of protection that are being provided at each tier of free or premium options. There are several excellent free anti-virus and network protection solutions on the market, but you should properly research and compare the offerings before deciding. Some recommended choices include Kaspersky, BitDefender, and Avast (Rubenking 2021).

The future of cybersecurity

Cyberthreats and cyberattacks are increasing at a high rate. This can be attributed to the increasing number of businesses who are migrating their services to the cloud, making it ‘faster, cheaper and easier than ever to put services online and collect huge amounts of data’ (Stone 2021, para. 8).

Fortunately, cybersecurity technology and practices are also continuing to evolve. It is predicted that AI (Artificial intelligence) will have a key role in cyber protection over the next 10 years, with some experts believing that it may even be considered malpractice to not be using AI, especially where the medical industry is concerned (Stone 2021).

Moving into the future, the industry will begin to see the adoption of AI to aid or replace the traditional password which many individuals fail to manage with the appropriate level of concern. In a survey by SailPoint Technologies, 75% of respondents admitted that they reuse the same password across different accounts, with 47% also using the same password between their work and personal accounts, and 15% saying they would consider selling their workplace passwords to a third party (SailPoint, 2018).

Cognitive Authentication is one emerging method as a replacement for passwords. Instead, machine learning algorithms will be used to track and learn user behaviours, actions and routines to determine and authenticate the user. Combining biometrics with cognitive authentication and device-specific identities could see the beginning of the phase-out of traditional passwords (Burt 2019).

The impact of cybersecurity on everyday life

With the enormous number of services that we must create accounts for the pressure to produce and remember complex passwords can be overwhelming, which is why many people will reuse the same passwords over and over. Services that try to ’help’ you by forcing you to update your password become problematic when users change their passwords by simply making a variation of their existing password, and subsequently forgetting which version of their password they used for what account, resulting in them being locked out of services. This is a common issue with platforms such as Facebook,for example. When a user forgets both their Facebook login and their email login at the same time. It makes it impossible for recovery of the account. People end up making a new account and/or email, but their old account still exists online, and they now have no control over the content of it or its visibility.

The introduction of biometric security, such as fingerprint scanning and facial recognition, has helped to reduce some of the burden of passwords for everyday users. In addition to logging into devices such as phones or gaming consoles, the applications on these devices are also integrating biometric options as a shortcut to accessing accounts. This encourages users to create more complex passwords at the initial setup, knowing that they will not have to enter it every time they log in. They will still need to write down the passwords in a secure place for the times when they do need it, but password storing options available through the operating systems or browsers can help with this as well. Electronic password storing extensions are not a 100% guarantee that they will be safe, but some can offer additional features such as showing you how long it has been since you changed a particular password and notifying you when a website you have an account on has potentially been compromised, prompting to review and update. Additionally, two-factor and multi-factor authentication provide an extra layer of security, while similarly reducing some of the worry about losing access to an account if a password or username is forgotten.
