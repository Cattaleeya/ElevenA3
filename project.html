Project

Overview

Topic

Since the beginning of the Covid19 pandemic, sales of indoor plants have increased exponentially. While the trend of owning indoor plants was already on the rise, the extended isolation periods, and work-from-home orders due to Covid19 has compelled many individuals to take up ‘plant parenting’ as a hobby, for relaxation and enjoyment, as well as purifying the air in their homes, and creating a way to stay connected with nature while unable to leave the house (Pflughoeft 2021). This booming trend, also known as ‘the Millennial plant revolution’ (George 2020) has seen the rise of inexperienced but hopeful house-planters who are now becoming frustrated at the expense and time they are putting into their new plants only to have them wilt and die from overwatering or being scorched, and then trying multitudes of remedies in order to reverse the damage, creating a cycle of confusion, and not knowing what else to do (Sullivan 2021).

Additionally, as the world begins to open back up, these new ‘plant-parents’, along with existing indoor plant enthusiasts, are going to find themselves faced with the challenge of keeping their plants alive when they are no longer home as often to care for them. Holidays, work trips, and long workdays can all contribute to the accidental neglect of house plants, with many forgetting to keep up with the watering, or returning home from a trip to dehydrated or dead plants. This leaves people needing to find a way to revive their plants or buy new plants each time they accidentally kill one.

To prevent the untimely demise of house plants, we are creating an automated indoor plant-care system that will allow wireless monitoring and watering of potted plants, with both manual and automatic settings. It is to be designed in such a way that allows you to easily accommodate different sizes and types of plants without needing to be an expert gardener. This will be especially beneficial to those wanting to ensure their plants are cared for while they are away from home, but also serve as an everyday set-and-forget care system, giving you one less thing to worry about.

By utilising and expanding on existing irrigation systems and open-source plant-care software we can create a product that will become an essential part of indoor gardening and remove the worry of trying to find a plant-sitter or having to leave your plants unattended when you are away for extended periods of time.

Motivation

An age-old problem with a modern solution. Plant death at the hands of neglectful or over-eager plant lovers is not a new concern, but the recent resurgence of pandemic-induced indoor gardening has brought the issue to the forefront. As people return to their busy lives, they are realising they can no longer be as attentive to their green life as they once had. This is where modern technology can help. The use of IoT (Internet of Things) devices in the home are increasing as consumers are realising the benefit of automated assistance. We believe our product, while it may seem like a novelty at first, could become a regular addition to households of today, and the future.

Landscape

There are several indoor automated watering systems and plant care apps on the market today, but none combine all the aspects that the user ideally wants in an all-in-one system. At present, automated indoor irrigation systems consist of a control box with one or two watering lines that may be split among multiple plants. 

The problem with these types of systems is that all plants (or groups of plants) must be watered at the same intervals with the same amounts of water. Additionally, while some systems on the market now come with an app to control them, if you require more than two watering schedules you are required to purchase and pair additional irrigation units, which can become expensive if you have a variety of plants with different needs. 

Another issue with this method of multiple drip irrigation is that they lack moisture sensors, and water level sensors, so not only will the pump continue once the water is gone, but you may accidentally overwater your plant, making it susceptible to root rot and fungus gnats. These systems also require some assembly by the user, such as measuring and cutting the lengths of tubing, attaching t-connectors and water filters, as well as needing to provide their own water reservoir.

With our system, a user can pot their plant directly into our smart pot, fill the built-in water reservoir, and then control how much and how often their plant will get water, depending on the plant’s individual needs. The user will also be able to keep their plants in any position around the home, instead of needing to group them all together in one place.

Project Details

The aim of this project is to create an indoor multi-plant irrigation system that can be easily monitored and controlled from a smart device, allowing users to care for their plants with differing needs while away from home.

Plans & Progress

In the beginning, our project started as an app-controlled multi-plant main station irrigation system that required the pot plants to be placed at/on or near the main station in order for a moisture sensor and drip line to be manually inserted into the pot. The main station would have a large reservoir in order to deliver water to each of the pots, at a rate that was unique to the needs of the individual plants in each pot, as opposed to delivering the same amount of water to all pots at the same time. The limitations of this were needing to decide on a set number of pots that could be accommodated, and how close the pots would need to be.

We then had the idea of adding an optional SmartPot that could water itself from a built-in reservoir while still sending data to the main station and allowing it to be controlled by the app, along with the individual plants at the main station. This would allow a plant to remain in its original position around the home, while its built-in waterlines and sensors mean that the user does not need to worry about inserting sensors, potentially damaging the roots of their plants, or not placing it deep enough to get an accurate reading. The user would just need to make sure the pot was charged before leaving and fill the pot’s water reservoir. Additional features considered for the project included light sensors and overhead lighting attached to the main station and pots so that plants could still receive adequate light if a user had their blinds closed while they were away for extended periods. We also considered having the smart pots placed on a base at the main station, having the main station fill the pot reservoirs, and the possibility of the base being a wireless charging pad for the pots.

After some deliberation, we decided that for our given timeframe, the best option for now would be to bypass the main station and work on producing the pot and app, which can do exactly what we were aiming for.

The Pot

While both components of our project had us thinking of many potential features, we again looked at what we could realistically achieve and started planning for what the essential functions of both the pot and the app would need to be. We first started with a discussion, and sketches of what the pot design may look like, and then produced a diagram of what the functions and features of the pot needed to be (See Appendix A).

We agreed that for a user to want this in their home, it would need to have a minimal and modern style, and not look too different from an ordinary pot so that it would be suitable to match with almost any interior decor. For this, we decided that a sleek white pot with a woodgrain style base would be best. We also discussed the possibility of designing ‘sleeves’ that a user could purchase separately to update the colour or pattern of their pot, without needing to buy a new pot and repotting the plant.

Pot Components & Design

The base section of the pot encases the batteries, Raspberry Pi and other electronic components in a water-tight recess that is accessed by a screw cap. The pump, water level sensor and tubing are housed by the inner wall cavity space that forms the 1.5L volume water reservoir. The moisture level sensor is inserted through the base of the inner pot wall, providing an optimal monitoring position at the base of the pot, and up into the plant roots.

The reservoir is filled by removing the top rim of the pot, allowing filling from any side. Water is distributed through two lengths of tubing that emerge at the top ring of the pot, on opposite sides, enabling water to be dispersed evenly and directly to the plant soil.

The pot is equipped with a USB charging port, and multi-colour LED power button, which indicates the status of the pot. (See Appendix B for detailed components diagram)

The App

When discussing the development of the app we had many ideas for features, some were not realistically within our scope, and some were possible future additions, including:

  • adding a social element to it via public user ‘greenhouses’ and the ability to follow other app users plant collections, or save plants they like to favourites list
  • a database of plants that a user can use to identify their plant and receive a recommended watering schedule
  • the ability to identify plant issues via a Plant Doctor feature which would tell the user what might be wrong with their plant, and how they can remedy it
  • the integration of ads and business profiles that allowed users to shop for their plants

When designing the app we started with a diagram plan of what features and functions it should have, and a Minimum Viable Product outline, for both App and Pot, to establish what the minimum requirements would be (See Appendix E). We then produced a roughly sketched wireframe design for a couple of ‘screens’ for the app, to see what it may look like and to explain ideas for functions. Both plans were created using a free InVision account.

Using Canva Pro we produced the logo, and the first 12 screens of a coloured mock-up idea, showing the main functions of the app, such as the login and sign-up screen, how to add a plant and how to connect a pot.

See the Appendix for product logo and user profile page mockup (Appendix F), and the full 12 mockup screens (Appendix G)

Market Research

When conducting market research we found that existing products either have a good app with or without a wireless moisture sensor component and no physical watering capabilities; an app but only controlling a unit that waters one or more plants but only at the same time and volume with no customisation, or they are a stand-alone irrigation unit also with scheduling set directly on the unit but also with only one (or in some cases two) pumps to accommodate only one or two watering schedules. Our product stands out here, by allowing customised monitoring and watering schedules for each pot that is paired.

Pricing on these units ranged from around $50 to $200. (See Appendix H for comparison table)

Testing

The project has multiple sections which will all need testing. The testing types will be both practicable and theory based. Described below are the sections and the type of testing we will be conducting.

1. App Development

2. Smart Pot Development

App Development

App development will require both theory and practicable testing programs.

App development theory test:

This will entail the design of the app and its functions. The app is to be tested internally with team to determine the correct flow and design of the app with user experience and functionality as priority.

App development Practical test:

This will entail the actual programming and execution of the app at runtime. The app is to be tested to ensure all errors in the code are handled and that the app ‘works’. This will be conducted in the later stages of the project development.

Smart Pot Development

The Smart Pot development will require several stages of testing like the app development.

Design theory testing:

This will require the team to assess the design of the smart pots and make decisions based on practicality, feasibility, and market competitiveness. The team will assess each function and component of the design, discussion the priority of the feature establishing which functions are core to the projects ideology and success then adding on functions until the criteria is met.

Smart Pot Design evaluation:

This will require the team to assess the look of the product to ensure the design guidelines have been followed and that the ideology remains intact. The group will assess the design and discuss any changes to be made based on referencing the project design guidelines.

Smart Pot practical test:

This will require the smart pot to be active and running both with the app and without the app finished. The purpose of this test is to make sure the pot is fully reactive with the sensors and functions as per the design guidelines. Electrical circuits, water flow systems and the sizing will be evaluated and changes are to be made based on findings from the test. A discussion will be held by which the team will be able to talk about the smart pot and add any concerns or solutions to the testing activity before passing the testing stage.

Risks

Hardware assembly parts becoming discontinued from supplier

Currently the parts required to create the Smart Pot are all readily available. There are some parts that are more at risk of discontinuation or being outdated. These parts include:

  • Raspberry Pi Zero
  • Rechargeable battery packs
The solution to the Raspberry Pi risk is the purchase adequate stock and then adapt design to suit updated model The solution to the battery pack is the buy adequate stock and then invest in alternate options for batteries that will suit the design.

App development functionality not achievable

A risk to developing apps are the design ideas themselves and the way they interact with the creation process. This is a huge risk to the project where if core functionality is too difficult, or unobtainable to begin with, then the project cannot be completed according to the design guidelines and project ideology. Therefore, it is important to research and confirm the ability to create the functionality within the app and the hardware to suit. An example of this would be:

  • The app will be able to identify what plant you have by the look of the plant
While this example might sound amazing, simplistic and ‘easy to execute’, in reality, is quite difficult to actualize making this simple idea a risk to the project success.

App Security – Data base breaches, Usernames, locations

Data security is an ongoing risk that will need to be managed throughout the whole project. Specifically, the risk of a data breach. The app will collect private personal information about people and it is important that the information is protected. Information that might be sourced through data breaches would include:

  • Usernames and passwords
  • Account information with personal information
  • Access to application and usage data
  • Payment information
  • Location services information
To protect the App against these types of breaches we will use well designed security protocols and programs in line with industry standards.

Group Processes & Communication

Our team maintains communication through weekly team meetings, group chat, and document collaboration via MS Teams, Microsoft Office and InVision. In the beginning, our team held weekly video calls to discuss project progress, but we adapted our communication schedule to include an additional informal meeting near the end of the week to provide further discussion, clarification, and updates on how tasks were progressing, allowing any issues to be resolved before the next official project meeting. Attendance at informal end of week meetings was not compulsory, but all team members were encouraged to attend if they were able. Team members who were working on tasks that involved one or two others were also able to schedule their own additional task-specific meetings. When collaborating on documents in Microsoft Office and InVision, team members communicated using positioned comments that highlighted the area for discussion. The comments were used to provide feedback, make suggestions, and ask for clarification on different aspects of the writing or diagrams in use. This allowed us to work together on the same item without needing to be in a call with one another.
